# Punctuation Guide — Novel Writing Standards

Use this guide when drafting, revising, or reviewing any chapter. Punctuation must serve the story's rhythm and clarity — never dominate it. The goal is a balanced, clean, and professional writing style suitable for a published novel.

---

## Core Principle

**Punctuation supports readability and rhythm. It should not call attention to itself.**

Prefer clear sentence construction and varied sentence length over heavy punctuation. Strong writing speaks through well-chosen words, not through punctuation signals.

---

## Mark-by-Mark Rules

### Em Dash (—)

**Use sparingly. Reserve it for:**
- A sudden interruption in dialogue (one speaker cuts off another)
- A sharp, unexpected shift in thought mid-sentence
- Strong emphasis that no other punctuation can convey

**Avoid em dashes:**
- In normal narrative flow to replace commas or colons
- As a default "dramatic pause" tool
- More than once or twice per page

**✅ Correct use:**
> "I never said I was going to—"  
> "Stop." Lev's voice was flat.

**❌ Overuse:**
> She was tired — exhausted, really — and the city — this vast, indifferent city — felt like it was pressing down on her.

---

### Comma (,)

Use commas for:
- Natural pauses within a sentence
- Separating items in a list
- Setting off descriptive or relative clauses
- Joining two independent clauses with a coordinating conjunction (and, but, so, yet, for, nor, or)

Do not use commas as substitutes for stronger punctuation when a full stop would serve the moment better.

---

### Period (.)

Use periods to create strong, clean sentence endings — especially for dramatic, impactful, or emotionally significant moments. A short sentence ending in a period lands harder than the same thought buried in a comma-heavy construction.

**✅ Use deliberate short sentences for impact:**
> The door was open. She hadn't left it that way.

---

### Colon (:)

Use colons to:
- Introduce an explanation or elaboration
- Introduce a list (when preceded by a complete clause)
- Signal an important revelation or turning point

Do not use colons mid-sentence where a comma would be more natural.

---

### Semicolon (;)

Use semicolons **only** to link two closely related independent clauses where the relationship between them is tight and the full stop would feel like an interruption.

> He didn't speak for a long time; the silence was answer enough.

Avoid chains of semicolons. If you find yourself using more than one per paragraph, restructure the sentences.

---

### Ellipsis (…)

Use ellipses **only** for:
- Hesitation, uncertainty, or trailing off in emotional dialogue or internal monologue
- A voice that loses courage mid-sentence
- An unfinished thought in a moment of grief, fear, or vulnerability

**✅ Correct use:**
> "I just thought that maybe… I don't know."

**❌ Avoid:**
> The stars were beautiful… the night was quiet… everything felt still…

Ellipses in narrative prose (outside dialogue or internal thought) typically weaken pacing. Prefer restructuring the sentence.

---

### Exclamation Point (!)

Use very rarely. One well-placed exclamation point carries weight; five in a chapter become noise. Prefer strong verbs and active construction over exclamation points to convey urgency or surprise.

---

## Quick Reference Table

| Mark | Primary Use | Frequency |
|------|-------------|-----------|
| Em dash (—) | Interruption, sharp shift, rare strong emphasis | Sparingly (< 2 per page) |
| Comma (,) | Pause, clause separation, lists | As needed |
| Period (.) | Clean endings, especially impactful moments | Liberally, including short sentences |
| Colon (:) | Introduce explanations, lists, revelations | Purposefully |
| Semicolon (;) | Link two tightly related independent clauses | Occasional |
| Ellipsis (…) | Trailing dialogue, hesitation, emotional uncertainty | Emotional moments only |
| Exclamation (!) | Genuine surprise or urgency only | Very rarely |

---

## Checklist: Punctuation Review

When revising or reviewing a chapter, check:

- [ ] Em dashes are used only for interruption, sharp shifts, or indispensable emphasis — not as general connectors
- [ ] No more than one or two em dashes per page
- [ ] Ellipses appear only in dialogue or internal monologue during emotionally charged moments
- [ ] Short, punchy sentences use periods — not ellipses — for dramatic effect
- [ ] Commas are not overloaded (restructure long comma chains into shorter sentences)
- [ ] Semicolons are used only where both clauses are genuinely independent and closely related
- [ ] Colons introduce something of weight: an explanation, a list, a revelation
- [ ] No stacked exclamation points
- [ ] Punctuation overall feels invisible — it guides the reader without announcing itself
